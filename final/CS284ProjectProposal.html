Foveated Rendering for VR Asynchronous Timewarp Operation

CS284A Final Project - Project Proposal
Cheol Jun Cho, Prashanth Ganesh, Cecil Symes

Summary

Asynchronous Timewarp describes a common VR operation which involves warping part of a fully rendered frame before displaying it, in order to account for and correct any head movement that may have occurred in between the beginning of the frame render, and the time the frame finally reaches the VR display (a metric known as motion-to-photon latency). By using foveated rendering to generate parts of the warped frame at a lower resolution outside of the fixation point (i.e. the fovea), we aim to decrease the time taken to warp the generated frame, allowing for faster display rates for VR headsets.

Problem Description

The VR rendering pipeline consists of a frame first being rendered by the “application” (i.e. the virtual world generator running on the CPU and GPU, responsible for creating the scene and generating the images displayed on the screen), then read from the frame buffer and displayed on the VR headset. However, in the time it takes for a frame to be rendered and displayed from the frame buffer, the user’s head orientation may have changed. This means that the displayed frame will be old, and slightly off from what should be displayed according to user motion. This slight difference interferes with the ideal user experience and precludes a true sense of presence in the virtual world.

The Asynchronous Timewarp [1] is a method for reducing this so-called motion-to-photon latency (the time it takes to emit photons to the screen from when the user turns their head). Not only can the timewarp use the current head position just ahead of the display refresh to generate an updated frame, it can also use prediction from the head tracking sensors to further improve the experience. The timewarp can be run asynchronously to the rendering of the original frame (hence the name) in order to further increase the perceived framerate. Specifically, the application engine can utilize the full capabilities of the graphics hardware without fear of missing a display refresh, because if that were to happen, the timewarp can use the last generated image to ensure that the deadline is met. As we can see, this operation should run as quickly  as possible to optimally reduce motion-to-photon latency. Generally, the original frame is rendered at full high-quality resolution and the warp operation uses a high quality filter of the original frame. Our proposal is to use the foveated rendering technique (i.e. reducing the image quality in the periphery of human vision) to reduce the latency of the asynchronous timewarp.

Goals & Deliverables

            

The goals of this project are reducing latency of asynchronous timewarp and providing more realistic, high quality rendering of VR scenes.

Performance Metrics

It is critical for VR programs to maintain a high framerate in order to be convincing for the user, and prevent nausea. As such, the performance metrics would be directly related to the framerate of the program, measured in frames per second, or Hertz. We will also measure the per-frame execution time in milliseconds. This can be compared head-to-head with the baseline implementation.

Baseline Deliverables

The immediate deliverable is to demonstrate the accuracy and efficacy of our proposed improvement to asynchronous timewarp when compared to the baseline implementation of the algorithm. We will complete this improved version of the ATW kernel and examine the latency, throughput, and quality tradeoffs of our proposed technique using different levels of foveation as well as different platforms (desktop versus embedded). This will be illustrated through plots demonstrating speedup on a handful of commonly used VR benchmarks (Sponza, Materials, and Platformer) using the poses and camera/IMU data from Vicon Room 1 Medium dataset (this has been used in the prior work ILLIXR [2]).

Aspirational Deliverables

Our vision beyond this primary deliverable is to demonstrate the improvement our kernel shows within the context of the current state-of-the-art XR testbed, ILLIXR [2]. This work showed that the original version of this ATW algorithm misses the maximum per-frame execution time needed for the target frame rate on several of these benchmarks. Our goal then is to replace their ATW kernel with ours and demonstrate an improvement over their results. This involves installing ILLIXR on a desktop and integrating our algorithm into its flow.

Additionally, our baseline deliverables assume that the gaze position of a user moves synchronously with the movement of the head. This is relevant because it also assumes that the foveated region remains the same. However, this may not necessarily be the case, as the eye tracking data has been shown to have unique patterns amongst different users [3]. One of our aspirational deliverables is to gain access to the dataset used in [3] to determine if a) our assumption holds or b) eye-head movement behaviors differ enough between users to warrant the use of different methods of foveated rendering to deliver the most realistic images on the screen.

Furthermore, the Asynchronous Spacewarp [4] builds upon the ATW to account for motion of the objects within the scene itself (not just user head motion) to further smoothen the experience and reduce CPU/GPU demand. We would like to investigate if there are related approximation techniques similar to foveated rendering that can be used in ASW to achieve a similar speedup.
